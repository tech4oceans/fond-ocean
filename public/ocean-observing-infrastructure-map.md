# Ocean Observing Infrastructure Map

![preview](../assets/public-previews/ocean-observing-infrastructure-map.svg)

This map highlights the core public infrastructures that make the ocean measurable, shareable, and increasingly understandable at planetary scale.

## Why This Layer Matters

Ocean Fund needs to understand not only stories and organizations, but also the observation backbone: the systems that produce data, coordinate platforms, map the seabed, organize biodiversity records, and turn scattered measurements into public intelligence.

## Core Infrastructures

- [GOOS](https://goosocean.org/what-we-do/) — the global ocean observing system led by IOC-UNESCO with a sustained, coordinated observing mission.
- [Argo](https://www.aoml.noaa.gov/proj/argo/) — the global float network measuring temperature, salinity, and increasingly broader ocean properties.
- [OceanOPS](https://goosocean.org/who-we-are/observations-coordination-group/oceanops/) — the operational hub that monitors and coordinates observing networks and metadata flows.
- [Copernicus Marine](https://marine.copernicus.eu/about) — a major public service for ocean state information, forecasting, and marine-climate intelligence.
- [EMODnet](https://emodnet.ec.europa.eu/en/about_emodnet) — the European marine data service bringing together bathymetry, geology, physics, chemistry, biology, habitats, and human activities data.
- [OBIS](https://obis.org/about/) — the Ocean Biodiversity Information System, a global biodiversity knowledge infrastructure for marine life occurrence data.
- [Seabed 2030](https://seabed2030.org/our-mission/) — the global mission to map the entire ocean floor by 2030.

## What Ocean Fund Should Learn Here

- how observing systems become trusted long-term infrastructure;
- how physical, chemical, biological, and mapping data are coordinated;
- how public data systems support climate, warnings, biodiversity, and ocean health;
- how ocean intelligence depends on both ships and satellites, sensors and standards, metadata and governance;
- how this Earth-ocean infrastructure can inform thinking about ocean worlds and planetary-scale observation.

## Ocean Fund Direction

Ocean Fund should treat observing infrastructure as one of its central public knowledge layers. Without this layer, indexes, ratings, articles, and public interpretation remain shallow. With it, the fund can build stronger rankings, atlases, essays, and educational routes that rest on real observing architecture.
